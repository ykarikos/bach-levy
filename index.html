About the record

With rapid shifts in musical ideals during Johann Sebastian Bach’s lifetime (1685–1750), the viol or viola da gamba was falling into oblivion. In this light, it is remarkable that Bach’s three surviving Sonatas for viol (BWV 1027–1029) are not typical 17th-century suites for solo viol and continuo but instead represent an 18th-century genre to whose development Bach himself contributed. In this sonata genre, the keyboard is not merely an accompanying instrument but an equal solo partner to the melody instrument. In fact, Bach’s viol sonatas are trio sonatas where the keyboard player is responsible for both the second solo part and the accompaniment.

Bach took the concept a step further in his organ Sonatas (BWV 525–530): the organist plays both solo parts on different manuals and the accompanying bass on the pedals. Though incorporating features of the Italian concerto, structurally these works are trio sonatas. In our own time, they have been successfully arranged for a variety of chamber ensembles. The viol is particularly suited to the extensive range they demand, hence the idea of arranging the six organ Sonatas for viol for this recording.

Such arranging and re-orchestrating was not unusual in the Baroque era. Bach himself was fond of reusing his earlier compositions in suitable contexts. He might adapt a Vivaldi violin concerto for organ, or convert a solo violin piece of his own into an obbligato organ part in a cantata chorus, to mention just a few examples. We do not know to which extent the organ Sonatas might be arrangements themselves, as a remarkable portion of Bach’s music remains lost to us. We do know that the first movement of the E minor Sonata is based on the opening of the second part of the Sinfonia of Cantata no. 76 and that the slow movement of the D minor Sonata was used later in the Triple Concerto BWV 1044.

Such reworking is often, with justification, seen as driven by necessity: a busy composer-craftsman recycling his earlier music under the stresses of multiple duties and time constraints. However, if we look at Bach’s surviving output as a whole, we realise that arranging and re-arranging was standard practice for him. He was obviously fond of revisiting musical materials in different forms, returning in some cases decades later to revise an early work or to set it in a completely new context. In some cases, he expended considerable time and effort on the re-working.

The organ Sonatas were brought together late in Bach’s life, when he returned to his earlier works for instance by organising some of his instrumental works in collections of Renaissance wind instruments in Sonus Borealis, an early music instrument and vocal ensemble which he founded in 1999 and continues to conduct. The ensemble released its first disc in August 2008.

Jaakko Saarinen
